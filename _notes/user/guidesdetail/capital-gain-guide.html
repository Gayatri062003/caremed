Capital gain guide

Capital Gain

1. What is “Capital Gain”?

Capital Gain is defined as any profit or gain that arises from the sale of a ‘capital asset’. This capital gain is charged to tax in the year in which the sale or transfer of the capital asset takes place.


Capital Asset

2. What is the meaning of Capital Asset?

The term “Capital asset” means:

  • Immovable assets such as Land, building or House property
  • Financial assets such as stocks, bonds, mutual funds
  • Intangible assets such as Trade marks, brand name, goodwill, copyrights, patents, technical know-how relating to the production of goods and services
  • Movable assets such as Gold,jewelry
  • Archaeological collections, drawings, paintings, sculptures; orany work of art
  • Any rights in or in relation to an Indian company including rights of management or control or any other rights whatsoever
  • Any securities held by a Foreign Institutional Investor

3. Are all assets defined as Capital Assets?

The following assets are excluded from the definition of Capital Assets

  • Any stock-in-trade, consumables or raw material held for the purpose of business or profession
  • Personal effects such as clothes and furniture held for personal use.
  • Agricultural land in rural India
  • 6½% gold bonds, 1977or 7% gold bonds, 1980or national defense gold bonds, 1980 issued by the central government
  • Special bearer bonds, 1991
  • Gold deposit bond issued under the gold deposit scheme, 1999
  • Deposit certificates issued under Gold Monetization Scheme, 2015

4. I own agricultural land in rural India, is it considered a Capital Asset as per the Income Tax Act?

For the purpose of exclusion as a Capital Asset, rural Area is defined as follows:

From A.Y. 2014-15-Any area which is outside the jurisdiction of a municipality or cantonment board, having a population of 10,000 or more.

Further, if it does not fall within a distance (to be measured aerially) given below – (population is as per the last preceding census).

2 kms from local limit of municipality or cantonment board If the population of the municipality/cantonment board is more than 10,000 but not more than 1 lakh
6 kms from local limit of municipality or cantonment board If the population of the municipality/cantonment board is more than 1 lakh but not more than 10 lakhs
8 kms from local limit of municipality or cantonment board If the population of the municipality/cantonment board is more than 10 lakhs

Short Term Capital Gains


Short Term Capital Gains

5. What are Short-Term Capital Assets?

An asset is defined as Short Term Capital Assetif it held for less than 36 months. An asset other than a Short-Term Capital Assetis regarded as a Long-term capital asset However, in certain cases the holding period of STCA is defined as follows:

  • Asset held for less than 24 months in the case of immovable property being land, building, and house property
  • Asset held for less than 12 months in the following cases:
    • A. Equity or preference shares in a company listed on a recognized stock exchange in India
    • B. Securities (like debentures, bonds, govt. securities etc.) listed on a recognized stock exchange in India
    • C. Units of UTI, whether quoted or not
    • D. Units of equity oriented mutual fund, whether quoted or not
    • E. Zero coupon bonds, whether quoted or not

6. What is Short-Term Capital Gain?

Profit from the sale of a Short-Term Capital Assetis termed as Short-Term Capital Gain.


7. What is the tax rate onShort-Term Capital Gains? How can I save Short-Term Capital Gains tax?

Short term capital gains are added to the total income and you need to pay income tax as per your income tax slab. For example, if Simran is in the 30% tax bracket, her short-term capital gains will be taxed at 30%.

Tax on short-term capital gain if securities transaction tax is applicable:

If securities transaction tax is applicable, the short-term capital gain is taxable at the rate of 15% +surcharge and education cess.


8. How to calculate short term Capital Gains?

Short Term Capital Gain = Sale Price – (Cost of Acquisition + Cost of Improvement + Expenses on Transfer)

Let’s explain this with the help of an example

Simran purchased a residential house in January 2017 for Rs. 20,00,000. She sold the house in January 2018 for Rs. 30,00,000. Expense on Transfer was Rs. 20,000.

She held the capital asset i.e. residential house for less than 24 months, hence the asset is a Short-Term Capital Asset.

Short Term Capital Gain = 30,00,000 being Sale Price – (20,00,000 being Cost of Acquisition +Rs. 20,000 being Expenses on Transfer) Short Term Capital Gain =Rs. 9,80,000


9. What is the meaning of Expenses on Transfer in relation to a capital asset?

Expenses on Transfer or Cost of Transfer include any expenditure incurred, whether directly or indirectly, for the purpose of transfer/ sale of a capital asset. Following are some examples of Expenses on Transfer:

Advertisement expenses

  • This includes cost of inserting advertisements in newspapers or on the internet for sale of the asset and commission paid to auctioneer, etc.

Brokerage or commission paid

  • This includes Brokerage or commission paid to broker for securing a purchaser in the case of sale of immovable property being land, building, and house property, or
  • Broker’s commission related to thesale of shares in the market where transaction has taken place through a broker

Stamp duty, registration fees

  • The transfer of immovable property being land, building and house property attracts stamp duty and statutory registration fees.

Legal expenses

  • legal expenses incurred for preparing conveyance and other documents

Travelling expenses

  • This includes expenses to get to the place where the asset is located in order to complete the transfer procedure. In case of house property there is a need for the registration which will require the presence of the buyer and in such a situation the individual will have incurred the expense to travel for this purpose.

Long Term Capital Gains


10. What are Long-Term Capital Assets?

An asset is defined as a Long-Term Capital Asset (LTCA) if it is held for more than 36 months. In other words, an asset other than a Short-Term Capital Asset is regarded as a Long-term capital asset.

However, in certain cases the holding period of LTCA is defined as follows:

  • Asset held for more than 24 months in the case of immovable property being land, building, and house property
  • Asset held for more than 12 months in the following cases:
    • A. Equity or preference shares in a company listed on a recognized stock exchange in India
    • B. Securities (like debentures, bonds, govt. securities etc.) listed on a recognized stock exchange in India
    • C. Units of UTI, whether quoted or not
    • D. Units of equity oriented mutual fund, whether quoted or not
    • E. Zero coupon bonds, whether quoted or not

11. What is Long-Term Capital Gains Tax

Profit from the sale of a Long-Term Capital Asset is termed as Long-Term Capital Gain.


12. What is the tax rate on Long-Term Capital Gains?

Long-Term Capital Gains are taxed at the flat rate of 20% + surcharge and education cess. In the budget 2018, long term capital gains on sale of Equity shares / units of equity oriented fund if more than Rs 1 lakh will be taxed at @ 10% without the benefit of indexation.


13. How to calculate short term Long-Term Capital Gains?

For Long-Term Capital Gains, you can also take benefit of Cost Inflation Index. Since, we can take indexation benefits, long term capital gains can be calculated by the formula:

Long Term Capital Gain = Sale Price – (Indexed Cost of Acquisition + Indexed Cost of Improvement + Expenses on Transfer+ Exemptions)

Cost Inflation Index

14. What is Cost Inflation Index ?

The Cost Inflation Index (CII) is a means notified by the Central Govt. to measure inflation. When you use CII for calculating long term capital gains, your gains are reduced by an amount - which is attributable to rising inflation. Just so you don't have to pay tax on your gains which have simply resulted due to inflation. Applying a CII factorlowers your gainssince the purchase price is adjusted for the impact of inflation.Thus, indexation helps reflect the actual value of the asset at present market rates.

15. How to compute Indexed Cost of Acquisition?

Indexed Cost of Acquisition = Cost of Acquisition x CII of the year of transfer CII of the year of purchase

Let’s explain this with the help of an example

Raj purchased a property in FY 2005-06 at Rs.20 lakh when Cost of Inflation Index or CII is 117 and sold the same in FY 2017-18 at Rs.60 lakh when CII is 272.

As Raj has held the property for more than 2 years, he can use Cost Inflation Index to bring down the tax. The indexed purchase price taking care of inflation using CII is

Indexed Cost of Acquisition or Purchase Price = (20,00,000 * 272)/117=Rs.46,49,572.65 (Around 46.5lakh)


16. How to compute Indexed Cost of Improvement?

Indexed Cost of Improvement = Cost of Improvement x CII of the year of transfer CII of the year in which the improvement was made

Let’s explain this with the help of Raj’s example above, If he had spent Rs. 1 lakh in FY 2012-13when CII is 200, the indexedcost of improvement taking care of inflation using CII is

Indexed Cost of Improvement = (1,00,000 * 272)/200=Rs.1,36,000 (1.36lakh)

Calculation ofLong Term Capital Gain inRaj’s case will be as follows:

Long Term Capital Gain (LTCG) = Sale Price – (Indexed Cost of Acquisition + Indexed Cost of Improvement + Cost of Transfer + Exemptions)

LTCG = 60 lakh – (46.5 lakh + 1.36 lakh) = Rs. 12.14 lakh


17. How to calculate Indexed Purchase price if property is purchased before 1 April 2001?

If the property is purchased before 1 Apr 2001 then the Fair Market Value (FMV) of the property as on 1 April 2001 can be considered as the cost of acquisition. Fair Market Value is the estimated price which any asset would ordinarily fetch if sold in the open market on the relevant date.


18. How to find Fair Market Value or FMV?

Fair Market Value or FMV shall be the higher of the cost of acquisition of the property or the price that the property shall ordinarily sell for if sold in the open market.

Some techniques that are used to calculate FMV are given below.

  • Compute the average sale price of similar properties in the neighbourhoodwhich were sold in the year 2001.
  • Determine the Circle rates prescribed for therelevant area. Note that these rates are fixed by the state government or the local development authority. The concept of circle rates came into existence for the first time in 1999 in Bihar. In other places like Delhi, it was adopted in 2007.
  • Consider real estate indices, such as the National Housing Bank’s (NHB’s) Residex, and two indices of the Reserve Bank of India (RBI)—Housing Price Index (HPI) and Residential Property Price Index (RPPI). These indices give an idea about the prevailing pricing trends in various cities. The indices are updated quarterly based on the revenue department’s transaction data.But the utility of these indices is limited.
  • Take help of a registered Valuation Officer Government-approved valuation officers follow a standard process for the valuation and provide a detailed report.They usually consider factors like dimensions of the property, freehold/leasehold, restrictive covenants (if any), whether insured or not.

In case of any enquiry, the income tax department will consider the value stated in the valuation report from a registered valuation officer

Tax saving on Capital Gains


19. How can I save taxes on Capital Gains?

You cannot avoid tax on short-term capital gains. However, you can claim deductions to lower the tax liability on long-term gains. You can save 100% of LTCG tax under various sections of the Income Tax Act such as section 54,54B, 54D, 54EC, 54EE, 54F, 54G, 54GA, 54GB.


20. How can I save Long term Capital tax on Sale of Residential Property?

Section 54: Exemption on Capital Gain on Sale of Residential Property by purchase / construction of another Residential Property

Under Section 54 – Any Long-Term Capital Gain, arising to an Individual or HUF, from the Sale of a Residential Property (whether Self Occupied or on Rent) shall be exempt to theextent such capital gain is invested in the

  • Purchase of another Residential Property within 1 year before or 2 years after the sale of property
  • Construction of Residential Property within a period of 3 years from the date of sale of property

However, If the new property is sold within a period of 3 years from the date of its acquisition the exemption under section 54 earlier will be withdrawn. The capital gain arising from this transfer will always be a short-term capital gain.


21. How to calculate exemption under section 54?

The amount of exemption under section 54 is least of the following:

  • i) Amount of Long-term capital gain, or
  • ii) Amount invested in the purchase or construction of the residential house

Let’s explain this with the help of an example:

Raj purchased a property in FY 2005-06 and sold it in FY 2017-18 for Rs.60 lakh.Long-Term Capital gain arising (after indexation) on sale of the house amounted to Rs. 13.50 lakh.

He purchased a piece of land for construction of a new house in FY 2017-18 for Rs. 10 lakhs.

Exemption under section 54 = least of the following:

  • i) Rs. 13.50 lakhs being Amount of Long-term capital gain, or
  • ii) Rs. 10 lakhs being Amount invested in the purchase or construction of the residential house

Taxable Long-Term Capital Gain (LTCG) = LTCG – Exemption under section 54 Hence, Taxable LTCG = Rs. 3.50 lakhs (i.e. Rs. 13.50 lakhs - Rs. 10 lakhs)


22. What is Capital Gains Account Scheme?

If you are unable to purchase/constructa new house property till the date of filing tax return (e.g. July 31), the capital gain would become taxable. To avoid the above situation, you can deposit the Amount of Capital Gain which is notutilizedunder the Capital Gains Account Scheme, before the due date of furnishing the return. The proof of such a deposit shall be attached with the Income Tax Return. The amount so deposited shall be eligible for exemption.

This scheme can be used under the following cases as well:

  • Section 54 F: Exemption on Capital Gain on Sale of the sale of any Long-Term Asset other than Residential Property
  • Section 54 B: Exemption on Capital Gain on Sale of land used for agricultural purposes

23. How can I save Long term Capital gain tax on Sale of Land or Building or both?

Section 54 EC: Exemption on Capital Gain on Sale of Land or Building by investing in Specified Bonds

Under Section 54 EC, Long-Term Capital Gain arising from the sale of any Land or Building (whether Residential or not) is exempt if it is reinvested inspecified bondsissued by the Central Government within a period of 6 months after the date of sale.

  • The eligible bonds are REC (Rural Electrification Corporation Ltd), PFC (Power Finance Corporation Ltd) and NHAI (National Highways Authority of India)
  • The maximum limit for investing in these bonds is Rs 50 lakh.
  • Tenure: Lock-in period of 3 years (Increased to 5 years From FY 2018­19) and non- transferable
  • Interest: 5.25 % payable annually. Taxable.

24. What are the consequences if the 54EC Bonds are sold/transferred or Converted into Money within 3 Years (5 years from FY 2018-19)?

  • The amount claimed as exempt under section 54EC shall be deemed to be long-term capital gain of the year in which the bonds are sold/transferred or converted into Money.
  • Note that If you take any loan or advance on the security of such bonds, they shall be deemed to have been converted into money on the date on which such loan or advance is taken.

25. How can I save Long term Capital tax on Sale of any Long-Term Asset other than Residential Property?

Section 54 F: Exemption on Capital Gain on Sale of the sale of any Long-Term Asset other than Residential Property

Under Section 54 F, any gain arising to an individual or HUF from the sale of any Long-Term Asset other than Residential Property shall be exempt in full, if the entire net sales consideration is invested in

  • Purchase of 1 residential house within 1 year before or 2 years after the date of sale of such an asset or in
  • Construction of 1 Residential House within 3 years after the date of such sale   In case the whole sale consideration is not invested and only a part of the sale consideration is invested, exemption shall be allowed proportionately i.e. 
  • Amount Exempt = Capital Gain x (Amount Invested / Net Sale Consideration)

26. How can I save Long term Capital tax on Sale of agricultural land?

Section 54 B: Exemption on Capital Gain on Sale ofland used for agricultural purposes

Under Section 54B, any gain arising to an individual or HUF from the sale of any agricultural landshall be exempt in full, if within a period of 2 years from the date of sale of the original agricultural land you purchase another agricultural land.

Note that the agricultural land should have been used for agricultural purposes during the 2 years immediately preceding the date of transfer/sale.


27. How to calculate exemption under section 54B?

The amount of exemption under section 54 is least of the following:

  • Amount of Long-term capital gain on sale/ transfer of original agricultural land, or
  • Amount invested in the purchase of new agricultural land

Let’s explain this with the help of an example:

Simran purchased an agricultural land in 2015. Since the date of purchase, the land was being used for agricultural purpose. The land was sold in July, 2017 for Rs. 20,00,000. Capital gain arising on sale of land amounted to Rs. 4,00,000. Out of the sale proceeds of old land, she purchased another agricultural land for Rs. 3,00,000 in 2017.

The amount of exemption under section 54B which can be claimed by Simran will be calculated as follows:

  • Rs. 4,00,000 being amount of Long-term capital gain on sale/ transfer of original agricultural land, or
  • Rs. 3,00,000 being amount invested in the purchase of new agricultural land

Thus, exemption will be Rs. 3,00,000. Taxable capital gain will come to Rs. 1,00,000 (i.e., Rs. 4,00,000 less Rs. 3,00,000).
